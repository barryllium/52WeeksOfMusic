I came for the punk rock, I stayed for the grooves.

* *The Clash* (1977)
* "Complete Control" (1977)
* "(White Man) in Hammersmith Palais" (1978)
* *Give 'Em Enough Rope* (1978)
* *London Calling* (1979)
* "I Fought the Law" (1979) <small>second playthrough only</small>
* "Armagideon Time" (1979) <small>second playthrough only</small>
* *Sandinista!* (1980)
* "Bankrobber" (1980) <small>second playthrough only</small>
* *Combat Rock* (1982)

I came into this week thinking "I'm going to like The Clash", right? I like punk rock, and I like alt rock. I knew a song or two, and I knew they had some highly regarded albums. And it didn't take long to feel comfortable, as halfway through "Janie Jones", the first song on *The Clash*, I was digging the beat, loving the music, and felt like I'd be alright. I didn't immediately love a specific song, but I liked the general vibe of the band, and was getting into each song. I could feel their influence in bands I like, like Green Day, Nirvana, Pearl Jam, and of course everyone's favorite punk band - [The Mr. T Experience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Mr._T_Experience). So after generally liking *The Clash*, I was excited for what came next.

And wow, did *Give 'Em Enough Rope* deliver. "Safe European Home" had such a cohesive feel between the music and the vocals, with great guitars and wonderful transitions as the music flowed. "English Cold War" took a familiar tune and turned it into a great beat with some awesome guitar playing. "Tommy Gun" has more great guitars and the drums kill it. And that's just the first three songs on the album, and it just keeps going. I found myself loving a song called "Drug-Stabbing Time" for its tremendous rhythm, good harmonies, and some subtle horns that stand out even more as the song goes on. I loved this album, and was wondering what I was getting into next with *London Calling*, an album I know to be considered foundational and one of the highest-regarded albums of all time.

So I started *London Calling*, and... I didn't love it as much as *Give 'Em Enough Rope*. I wanted more of the raw punk energy from their previous music. And don't get me wrong, I really liked many of the songs on the album, starting with "London Calling" and its fantastic Guitar -> Bass -> Drums -> Vocals intro, to the tremendous bass in "Lost in the Supermarket" (and its great guitar break), the opening piano followed up by the horns and drums of "The Card Cheat", and the run near the end of the album culminating in "Train in Vain (Stand By Me)". But it just didn't click for me on the first time through; didn't follow up on *Give 'Em Enough Rope* enough for me.

The second listen made a difference, though. I got it a bit more - the album took punk rock and evolved it. It brought other genres into punk in a way that no one else had. It was a foundational album that helped create the blueprint for alternative rock. My second listen helped me understand this more, and helped me understand the album's importance - even if I still didn't end up loving it as much as some of their previous music.

So how do you follow up an album like *London Calling*? With *Sandinista!*, a triple album that sprawls all over musically. If *London Calling* was "let's bring a handful of genres into punk", *Sandinista!* was almost "let's bring in the rest". Some of this really worked for me, and some of it didn't, but what worked really did work. The beat right off the bat in "The Magnificent Seven" was dope - bass, guitars, and drums sounded amazing. I even liked it when it got a little weird. "Somebody Got Murdered" had some great music with some fantastic transitions. "One More Time" and "One More Dub" are two versions of the same song - each bringing their own energy and style, both working in their own ways. One of my favorite examples on the album of what The Clash was doing on the album is "The Sound of Sinners". This is a gospel song, and somehow a punk song. It does an amazing job of being the type of song it's trying to be. Did I have "I liked a gospel song" on my bingo card this week (or even this year?)? Nope, but it happened.

*Combat Rock* closed out my week, and while it was a weaker album, it had the high point of "Should I Stay or Should I Go". This was the one song that I knew for sure was by The Clash, and I knew I liked it. Listening to it again did nothing to change that - the intro is great, it is one of the better uses of guitar distortion in a song that I've heard in a long time, and does a great job of transitioning through the song with subtle starts and stops. Classic song for a reason.

More than anything this week, I could feel the influence The Clash had on artists I love. Even though their earlier work was my favorite, there were things about their evolution that really worked for me and often impressed me. Their musical mastery was apparent - and they really worked as a unit for me. At no point did I feel like a single member of the group "made the band" - even though some instruments or vocal performances stood out from song to song, they really felt like a cohesive group. The Clash are near the top of the list of the artists I've listened to this year where I really felt their influence and their legacy.

* Favorite Album: No doubter for me, it's *Give 'Em Enough Rope*. Even though it doesn't hold the significance or legacy of albums like *The Clash*, *London Calling*, or *Sandinista!*, this album kept me pulled in from start to finish better than any of the rest. I have a feeling that if I listened to each album 10 more times, *London Calling* might sneak up there, but I'm still not sure it would pass *Give 'Em Enough Rope*.
* Favorite Song: This is tougher for me, there are a handful of songs near the top this week, all of which I've mentioned. But it goes to "Train in Vain (Stand By Me)". I love the music so much, and we all know I'm a sucker for songs with either a piano or harmonica - and this song has both. I love the staccato vocals in some of the verses, and the overall beat is just great.

I’m really interested in how next week goes, as I go from The Clash back to The Velvet Underground, one of the bands that helped shape the musical world they came out of.
