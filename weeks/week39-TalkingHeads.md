Entering this week, for some reason - even though I couldn't name a single song by the Talking Heads, I thought I knew what I was in for - I thought I knew what their music was like. Spoiler: I didn't.

* *Talking Heads: 77* (1977)
* *More Songs About Buildings and Food* (1978)
* *Fear of Music* (1979) <small>second playthrough only</small>
* *Remain in Light* (1980)
* *Speaking in Tongues* (1983)
* *Little Creatures* (1985)

I started the week hopeful, knowing that I was going to be listening to two of *Rolling Stone's* 2023 top 500 albums of all time. It was a dramatic turn three songs in, then, when I put in my notes "Getting a small amount of a Kinks vibe here...". Thankfully, it wasn't quite that bad - but after a few weeks in a row where I wasn't completely connecting with an artist, I found myself there again. There were things I liked - and I'll get to those - but I think I can sum this week up with "I just don't get it".

On one hand, this week should have been right up my alley. A neurodivergent lead singer/songwriter writing a lot of songs about not understanding society and feeling left out - that should absolutely work for me, right? And, on a handful of songs, it did - songs like "No Compassion", "The Girls Want to Be with the Girls", and "Seen and Not Seen" resonated a bit - even if I haven't quite had the same feelings/experiences, I could relate a bit to the social awkwardness/anxiety elements. But then there are songs like "Who Is It", "Stay Up Late", and "Drugs" that were just too weird for me, or I just couldn't even understand the purpose of the song or why it even exists.

The biggest disappointment for me was *Remain in Light*. This is a top-40 album on the *Rolling Stone* list, and I felt completely underwhelmed by it from start to finish. Yes, "Once in a Lifetime" has a ridiculously catchy chorus with some really good bass and percussion. But the verses/spoken word fell short for me. In many songs on the album, I was just overloaded by all the music that was happening, often feeling like the goal was to add even more percussion after they had added extra percussion to the existing percussion. After researching the significance of the album, I get the concept - it combines punk, funk, and Afrobeat. I'm aware of the fact that it influenced artists that I do like. But here's the thing, I just didn't like it.

But I do like to try to find something every week that I like, even if I really have to try. And I found it about halfway through my first listen to *Little Creatures*. My favorite part about this entire week was noticing and then really liking the work of Tina Weymouth, the bassist of Talking Heads.

I noticed the bass lines on several songs early, but the more I listened, the more I was drawn to those bass lines. And as I went back through in my second playthrough, I noticed that every time I was drawn into a song, it was either because of the bass playing, or the combination of the bass and drums. More often than not, even in songs where David Byrne's vocals pushed me away (I was mostly not a fan), I *really* liked the rhythm and the feel of the songs. Even in *Remain in Light*, when I was able to parse my way through the music, that bass/percussion rhythm was there - and it was almost always my favorite part of the song. There are a handful of moments where I really like the guitar, or the keyboards - and even a few songs where I really liked the vocals ("Take Me to the River", "The Big Country", "Pull Up the Roots", "This Must Be the Place (Naive Melody)", and "And She Was"). But that bass, it was great.

Honestly, just go listen to "Psycho Killer". This is a song that I have personally been mixed about for years, simply because of the "fa fa fa fa fa" lines being so prominent. But when I really paid attention to the bass this week, I "got it". Yes, the drums and guitar are also really good in the song - and it's one of Byrne's better performances - but the bass *makes* the song, it's excellent. "No Compassion", "I Zimbra", "Once in a Lifetime", "Burning Down the House", "Girlfriend is Better", "Stay Hungry", "I Get Wild / Wild Gravity", "This Must Be the Place (Naive Melody)", and "Take Me to the River" are all great examples of her work either making a good song even better or saving a song that otherwise wouldn't work for me at all. And it's not even like she's doing impossible things with the bass - she finds a great beat, and deploys it perfectly in a way that the song would be completely lost without it. She drives so many of the songs, and was easily my favorite part of this week.

Speaking of favorites...

* Favorite album: This came down to two albums for me - *More Songs About Buildings and Food* and *Speaking in Tongues*, and it goes to *More Songs About Buildings and Food*. It is overall a more consistent album, and ends strong with "Take Me to the River" and "The Big Country". The music is consistently good - with, of course, some great bass playing. And I was all in on the neurodivergence in "The Girls Want to Be with the Girls" and "I’m Not In Love" (which featured a great intro).
* Favorite song: Again, the aforementioned "Take Me to the River" - and this week's winner, "And She Was". The bass line is really good (and often subtle). There are some really great transitions where the bass, drums, and guitars kick in more. The vocals are some of Byrne's best for me, and there are some really great harmonies as well. And the horns and guitars in the last minute bring the song home great.

So here I am at the end of the week, where I was hoping to break out of a few weeks' rut... and I don't think I've done it. Yes, I found some things I liked, but there was too much that I just didn't get or like as well. Let's hope Joni Mitchell has something better for me next week.
