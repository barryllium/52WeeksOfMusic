Two weeks ago, I was looking forward to getting to Sonic Youth, because what I thought I knew about them made me think I'd like them a lot. Then I listened to The Velvet Underground last week (where I did not have a great experience) and learned that Sonic Youth was directly in their lineage. At that point, I became worried...

* "Shaking Hell" (1983) <small>second playthrough only</small>
* "The World Looks Red" (1983) <small>second playthrough only</small>
* "Death Valley '69" (1985) <small>second playthrough only</small>
* *EVOL* (1986)
* *Sister* (1987)
* *Daydream Nation* (1988)
* *Goo* (1990)
* *Dirty* (1992)
* "The Diamond Sea" (1995) <small>second playthrough only</small>
* "Incinerate" (2006) <small>second playthrough only</small>

Two albums in, and I was right back where I was last week at the same point - pondering whether it was worth listening to anything else. I actively did not like many of the songs, and the music was just not quite coming together for me. I felt like I was listening to someone's high school/college band - every band member was playing their heart out, thinking that *they* made the band; you can only kinda hear the vocals because the mix is bad and the music is overpowering. Through those first two albums, I found one song that I *maybe* liked ("Hot Wire My Heart"), and that was still only a maybe.

And then, thankfully, I started *Daydream Nation*. The very first song, "Teen Age Riot", felt like a completely different band. Yes, I could feel the progression through the first two albums to this point, but for the first time I heard music and vocals that complemented each other. I felt a coherence to the music, all the different instruments felt like they were working together. I went immediately from "I hate this band" to "oh, wait, I really like this song".

Not every song kept that momentum going - the next song ("Silver Rocket") goes off the rails halfway through, and the less said about the album ending with "Trilogy: z) Eliminator Jr.", the better. But along the way, song after song hit for me. "'Cross the Breeze" has a super catchy beat - especially once the vocals start - and transitions into a great chill mood near the end. "Hey Joni" has some killer music and transitions (and overcomes some sketchy vocals in a few places). The simple arrangement to start "Candle" works, and transitions so well when the music kicks in - and the vocals work well with the music. "Kissability" has some great drums, giving a great beat that I really got into. And "Trilogy: b) Hyperstation" offers a great journey with the music building up repeatedly in a way that kept pulling me into the song. After two albums where I struggled to pick out anything I liked, this album kept grabbing me and making me pay attention to almost every song on it.

Thankfully, this was not just a one-album wonder. *Goo* starts off with "Dirty Boots", and it was one of my favorite songs of the week. Its subtle drums/bass transitions throughout were great, and the song really kicks in halfway through with the vocals and guitars building well together. I liked a handful of other songs, with "Tunic (Song for Karen)" and "Disappearer" standing out the most. It was a solid album, not quite on the level of *Daydream Nation*, but I liked it so much more than *EVOL* and *Sister* - which was simple, because I strongly disliked those albums.

I ended my album run with *Dirty*, which started off slow for me. "100%" and "Swimsuit Issue" had different issues (too much guitar distortion in the former, forced sounding vocals in the latter) that pushed me out of the song. But there is a stretch in the middle of this album that I really enjoy. Most of "Shoot" feels great - with the exception of one vocal issue that I'll revisit shortly. Following that are "Wish Fulfillment" and "Sugar Kane", which were the best back-to-back songs of the week for me. The vocals and music pair really well in "Wish Fulfillment", and the music goes right up to the edge for me when it picks up, hitting that line perfectly without becoming "too much". The bass and guitar immediately pulled me into "Sugar Kane". The guitars and drums take it from there, and the song flows really well - it had one of my favorite beats all week. The rest of *Dirty* was solid, with a lot of songs that I liked, and a few that just fell short of me really liking them. But above all, this three-album stretch put me in a different place heading into my re-listen. I was no longer hating Sonic Youth - and I was thinking maybe I would even like the first few albums more on my second listen.

Spoiler - I still don't like those albums. I did, however, dislike them less. I found more musical things to hang on to, and while these are definitely not albums I would ever go back to, I didn't hate them this time around. And that sentiment goes for everything during my second playthrough - I liked everything a little bit more (and found a few singles that I kinda liked as well in "The World Looks Red" and "Incinerate"). More than anything, it helped me figure out what it was that I did and didn't like about Sonic Youth:

* Liked:
  * They would often put together some great beats, the drums and bass would settle into a great rhythm and the guitars would layer dynamism on top of it.
  * The few times they would harmonize, it would almost always sound good to me.
  * There are some "chill" moments in a lot of songs, where they would settle into a song and it was really effective.
  * They transition really well in and out of choruses and bridges. More often than not this was the most effective part of any song - a song would often completely change temporarily, and then snap back to where it was. Even if I didn't like all the parts, the transitions really stood out to me.
  * When the vocal mix is good, the vocals and music complement each other really well, building and fading together in an engaging way.
* Didn't Like:
  * I don't care if it means I'm old: often songs were just noise. For instance, go listen to "Scooter and Jinx", a song that to me felt like someone left a microphone on next to a race track and recorded cars for 1.5 minutes.
  * Spoken word songs: way too many songs where Thurston or Kim is just talking instead of singing. And I even like a few of the songs where they are *just barely* singing - but the songs where they just talked for a bit drove me nuts.
  * Kim Gordon's forced vocals: there are a few songs on *Dirty* where I honestly thought she was going to be sick while singing. And I like her vocals when she's singing normally, but the forced vocals were almost nauseating.

So, do I like Sonic Youth? Kind of. More than I liked The Velvet Underground, that's for sure. There are a few albums I'd consider revisiting, and a few songs I kind of liked. Overall, I came to appreciate their musicality, especially in their later work. But at the same time, I have a strong feeling that two weeks from now I won't be able to remember much about them, won't be able to say "I really liked this song for this reason". With that glowing review, time for some "favorites":

* Favorite Album: *Daydream Nation*, though *Dirty* comes close. *Daydream Nation* had a higher hit rate for me, and also a lower "Nope" rate.
* Favorite Song: Not a lot of contestants here, and it comes down to "Teen Age Riot" and "Sugar Kane", with "Sugar Kane" taking it. I just really liked the beat, and when it slows down to build back up in the final third, that moment worked for me better than any moment in any song this week.

And with that, I head into another week that I have trepidation about: Brian Eno.
