Time to enter the world of EDM. Or, as some would call it, [just M](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=CB_jadW1DCo&t=13s).

* *Homework* (1997)
* *Discovery* (2001)
* *Human After All* (2005)
* *TRON: Legacy* (2010) <small>second playthrough only</small>
* *Random Access Memories* (2013)

It was a scary start to the week - much like when I was a student, I mostly did not like *Homework*. The album was full of tracks that had a solid beat, but would either have too much repetition or a sound that I was worried would break my brain. Whether it was the tire screeching sounds in "Rollin' & Scratchin'" or the buzz in "Rock'n Roll", some sounds would just push me away from many songs. The most frustrating example was in "Burnin'" - there is this weird video-game-like laser sound that was happening every few seconds through the entire song, and it gets higher in pitch throughout parts of the song. I HATED that sound, but what made it frustrating is that I *really* liked everything else about the song, to the degree that a version of the song without that sound would have been in the running for my favorite song of the week. But, the sound flat out ruins the song for me, leaving "Around the World" as my favorite song from this album - possibly helped by nostalgia, since I very much remember this song from college. But even with its repeated vocal loop, the dynamic beat is fantastic and the keyboard synth is really good.

While I left my first playthrough of *Homework* wondering if I was not going to like Daft Punk at all, thankfully *Discovery* came along to disabuse me of that notion. In some ways, it was almost as if this was an album from a different artist. There were lyrics. There was a slight expansion of instruments (or, at least an expansion of what instruments were sampled). I immediately liked the first several tracks. "One More Time" has a great beat, an actual vocal performance, and some nice tempo changes - especially in the middle when the main beat drops and there are only vocals and the synth keyboard. "Aerodynamic" has some really great guitar playing (I think it's a synth, but still). Oddly enough, I found "Harder Better Faster Stronger" to just be "alright", given that it's one of the songs that people seem to know from Daft Punk. But the song I liked the most was "Something About Us". Starting with just the keyboard, then adding in guitar and bass, it has a great vibe that I really dug. Yes, the vocal effect is a little weird - but for the most part, that was just part of the deal this week.

So, surely, *Human After All* was going to continue the growth and evolution from *Homework* to *Discovery*, right? Sadly, no. It was almost as if I listened to two different artists this week, and *Human After All* was a journey back to the artist that seemed to actively be trying to break my brain. Sometimes it was what I can only describe as an "electronic belch" sound in "Human After All". Other times it was the vocoder effect being too much like in "The Prime Time of Your Life". Or it was an extraordinary amount of repetition like in "Steam Machine" or "Emotion". "Make Love" was the oasis on this album. It has a great feel, with the keyboards and synth guitar melding well together. If it were a three minute song, I would absolutely love it, and even at five minutes, I still liked it.

Now we take the quick aside into the "TRON: Legacy" soundtrack. This is how I mostly knew Daft Punk coming into this week. For the record, I actually like the movie (though it's not on the same level as the original *TRON*). If there's one thing I've heard people say, though, is that the soundtrack is great - and I agree. They really nailed the feel of the *TRON* world with the soundtrack, and while not every song is perfect, I felt myself getting lost in the soundtrack to a degree, as it "just worked" for me, with solid beats that pulled me back into that world.

Finally, though, we come to *Random Access Memories*. Which version of Daft Punk would I find here? Thankfully, the version that made *Discovery* was back! The number one thing that I loved about this album, right from track one, was the addition of more real instruments. Yes, the synths were still there, but the real instruments made such an amazing difference, and raised every track to something new, something even better. In some ways, it reminded me of how much more I loved Run-DMC songs more when they mixed more real instruments in with their drum machines and turntables. Song after song on this album included keyboards (piano and/or organ), guitars, bass guitars, and even full orchestras - and it made for some music. "Give Life Back to Music" and "The Game of Love" start the album of great, with both displaying some great keyboard and guitar playing. The album ends strongly with "Contact", a song with an epic feel that builds itself up well with great energy. In between, I enjoyed almost every song - with my favorite being "Within". Even with the vocal processing, it's an emotional track with beautiful piano backing it and strong lyrics. Remove that vocal processing, and I think I'd absolutely love the song, so the fact that I still really liked it should speak volumes to what I liked about it even with said processing.

I can't get away from this album without also talking about "Giorgio by Moroder". I'm not even sure what the first 5 minutes of this song is - it's mostly an interview set to music, and I didn't like it. But, once the orchestra kicks in for the last few minutes, this song can get it. Oh, hold on, what's this? I'm being handed a note. Let me read this…

> Please stop saying "can get it". We didn't appreciate it three weeks ago, and we don't appreciate it now. Because of you, we have all stopped saying it, and we kindly request that you stop as well. Thank you.
>
> Sincerely,<br />
> The youth of America

I gotta say, I did not see that coming… regardless, this song rocks once it actually is a song, and not a weird interview - which I guess makes it one of the most interesting songs of the week.

I can't say that I'm going to revisit Daft Punk a lot - it's not going to be a "I'm going to sit down and listen to Daft Punk" kind of group. But unlike the Grateful Dead, I get it. They have a lot of songs that I got into *physically* - sometimes I would be walking around while listening to them, and I would start "dance-walking" - almost strutting around getting into the beat. They had some great music (especially on *Discovery* and *Random Access Memories*), and a handful of songs that I fully enjoyed.

* Favorite Album: Almost a toss-up between *Discovery* and *Random Access Memories*. In the end, when I ask myself which album I could see myself revisiting in the future, it's *Random Access Memories*. It blends multiple genres, and the addition of real instruments put it over the top for me.
* Favorite Song: "Within" gets it. It was one of very few songs with emotionality to it, and that gave it the edge. On a strong album, it was the strongest song.
