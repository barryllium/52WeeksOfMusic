Last week, and here we are with St. Vincent. Appropriate for the last year, it was quite a journey.

* *Marry Me* (2007)
* *Actor* (2009)
* *Strange Mercy* (2011)
* *St. Vincent* (2014)
* *MASSEDUCTION* (2017)
* "Pay Your Way in Pain" (2021)

I have had a great run for the past month, and I was prepared for St. Vincent to rock my socks immediately. I knew she was in the vein of some artists that I really liked from this year: David Bowie, PJ Harvey, Prince, and others. And then I started to listen to *Marry Me*... and I was just not feeling it. I didn't hate what I was hearing, but I was expecting/hoping for something with a little more edge to it, like early PJ Harvey. That early rocking of my expectations took a little bit to shake off, but - thankfully - I came around. And it helped a lot that the further I got into her career, the more I connected with her music. A second listen helped as well.

*Marry Me* and *Actor* almost felt like rough drafts for her future albums to me. They are a good introduction to something that she does very well, but also had a lot of sharp edges for me that prevented me from completely connecting with many songs. One thing that jumped out immediately, even when the songs themselves weren't quite clicking for me yet, was how deliberately St. Vincent juxtaposes the sound of her music against what she's actually singing about. "Now, Now" off *Marry Me* is the clearest early example - it's got this candy-coated, almost bouncy feel to it, but the lyrics are sitting in a much darker, sadder place, and that mismatch is part of what makes the song work even when it nearly runs off the rails at the end. "Marry Me" itself does something similar, wrapping an emotional, almost aching vocal performance in a lush, pretty arrangement - piano, violin, horns, a drumbeat that works like a heartbeat - so the prettiness of the music never undercuts the vulnerability underneath it. By *Actor*, she's leaning into that trick even more explicitly. "The Strangers" didn't fully grab me, but it's a good statement of purpose - the gap between her vocal performance and the lyrics is clearly "her thing," and she's already doing it well. "Laughing With a Mouth of Blood" is where it really landed for me on that album: the song has a very good beat and a chorus that flat-out lands, strings and all, but it's paired with much darker lyrics, and it's one of the first times on these two albums where that contrast felt earned rather than just interesting. Even this early, before I was sold on the songs themselves, I could tell that sweetness-hiding-sadness was going to be a throughline for her.

That same willingness to let things clash, though, is also where a lot of those sharp edges show up. "Now, Now" is a good example of both sides of it - the candy-coated/dark-lyrics contrast works, but the song also just has too much going on by the end and loses me a bit. "All My Stars Aligned" has a piano and bass line that outright clash with each other, and it mostly works, but only just. *Actor* pushes that further, and not always successfully - "Marrow" has a great Prince-influenced groove going for two-thirds of the song and then goes off the rails in the final third with too much distortion, and "Actor Out of Work" almost sounds like two different songs layered on top of each other, which is interesting in theory but turns into a bit of a mess whenever everything is firing at once. "Black Rainbow" splits the difference - the distortion in its build-up both works for and against the song at the same time. So even as I was picking up on what she was going for thematically, the arrangements themselves hadn't fully caught up yet.

It's with *Strange Mercy* that everything starts to come together for me more. Right off the bat, "Chloe In the Afternoon," "Cruel," and "Cheerleader" show a coherence that was mostly missing across her first two albums. "Chloe In the Afternoon" catches me with its bass, and the music has surprising depth to it - guitar and keyboards/synth playing off each other, giving the song some bite that actually works with her voice instead of fighting it. "Cheerleader" is even tighter: the guitar/violin intro is almost entrancing, and there's a clear standout moment just before the one-minute mark where the percussion kicks in for the chorus - the vocals and music finally feel like they're pulling in the same direction. But "Cruel" is where the album really clicks for me. The guitar carries the song, her vocal delivery on the title word itself is a highlight, and the keyboards and violins sound good even in the moments they threaten to overshadow her voice. Her performance builds in strength heading into the chorus, and the orchestral rise that opens the final third is genuinely compelling - it's the first song of hers that fully landed for me, both times through.

The album continues to be strong the rest of the way, even after hitting its peak with "Cruel". "Strange Mercy" has a nicely restrained arrangement early on, with a very pure vocal performance - only to take a dark turn in the second half that feels entirely earned and matches the mood of the lyrics. "Champagne Year" provides a strong emotional performance combined with music that creates a palpable mood that subtly builds on itself throughout the song (I particularly love the sparse keyboards early in the song). The tail end of the album didn't hit as hard as the previous songs, but even the worst songs showcased her musical evolution and growth from her first albums.

If things really started to come together for me with *Strange Mercy*, it's with *St. Vincent* that they concretized. The clash that used to work against her - piano and bass fighting each other, arrangements that felt like they were competing with her voice instead of supporting it - is basically gone here. In its place is a rhythmic backbone that shows up again and again across the album: synth, moog, and drums locking in together to do most of the heavy lifting, with her vocals given room to sit on top of it rather than get buried underneath. There's also a much more overt Prince influence running through the record, and the "sweetness hiding something darker" trick from her earlier albums shows up again here too, just more assured. "Huey Newton" is the best example of it - the vocal performance is almost chilling in how light and airy it feels while it's actually saying something much darker, and the song earns that contrast in a way "Now, Now" or "Marrow" were only reaching for.

Three songs in particular stood out for me. "Prince Johnny" is the clearest showcase of that synth/moog/drums foundation - the mood it sets is immediate, her vocal performance is both emotional and vulnerable, and the guitar that shows up in the final third caps off a song that feels locked-in at every level. "Digital Witness" leans hardest into the Prince-like feel, with horns riding the drums and bass into a beat that's supremely catchy, and there's a real bump in energy about 40 seconds in when the chorus hits that makes the whole thing feel like a banger. "I Prefer Your Love" is the quietest of the three - synth and drums build an engaging, moving backdrop, the guitar is used sparingly but effectively, and the result is just a pure, lovely song built around a quietly powerful vocal performance. All three hit that same balance of a strong musical foundation giving her voice room to do exactly what it needs to.

Taken as a whole, this is an album I could easily see myself coming back to. Things feel cohesive in a way her first few albums didn't, her vocal performances are front and center rather than competing with the arrangements, and the whole thing complements itself throughout in a way that finally lived up to what I was hoping for going in.

*MASSEDUCTION* keeps a lot of that same Prince-inspired energy going, showing up directly on the title track and again on "Sugarboy," but it pushes even further into being a beat-first, rhythm-forward record - almost every song note I took comes back to the strength of its beat before anything else. There's also a structural trick she leans on a lot here: songs that shift gears partway through rather than staying in one place. "Pills" softens and transforms in its final third, "Los Ageless" builds through its chorus into a downright epic second half, and "Savior" has a couple of transitions right around its midpoint that change the whole feel of the song. It adds up to a solid album with a strong run through the middle, even if it doesn't grab me quite as completely as what came before it.

"Pills", "Happy Birthday, Johnny", and "New York" pulled ahead of the rest for me on *MASSEDUCTION*. "Pills" is catchy almost in spite of itself - it's repetitive to a fault, but the hook works, the guitar has some good moments, and that final-third turn where the song softens and rebuilds is one of the better moments on the album. "Happy Birthday, Johnny" goes the opposite direction: the arrangement stays simple and restrained on purpose, just enough small shifts happening between vocal parts to highlight how vulnerable her performance is - it's one of the few songs here that actually made me care rather than just admire. "New York" is the one that stuck with me most, though. It starts piano-led, and even those sparser moments work on their own, but the song fully comes alive as bass, percussion, and strings progressively layer in underneath her voice - by the end it's operating on a different level than most of the album. "Pills" (and "Smoking Section," a bit) also grew on me more the second time through, which says something about how much this album rewards sitting with it, even if it never quite overtook the album before it.

I can't say that I ended this week absolutely loving St. Vincent, but I did grow in my appreciation. She has a really good voice that reminded me of the parts of Kate Bush's voice that I most liked. She's a strong storyteller too - several songs ("Laughing With a Mouth of Blood", "Regret", "Severed Crossed Fingers") grabbed me simply because of a handful of lyrical moments; I'm confident that I would find a lot more I liked lyrically the more I listened to her. When she leaned more into the PJ Harvey, David Bowie, and Prince influences (and less into Brian Eno and Talking Heads), I found myself getting into her music. As I found myself liking her later work more and more, it definitely made me interested in checking out her more recent work sometime in the future.

* Favorite Album: Her last three albums were really strong, but *St. Vincent* takes this. It had a consistency to it that none of the other albums quite reached, and provided the highest number of candidates for favorite song. It had some of her best vocal performances, everything locked in together, and it would without-a-doubt be the first album I'd revisit.
* Favorite Song: This was less clearcut, as three different albums provided a strong candidate here. "Digital Witness" takes it over "Cruel" and "New York": that chorus kick still gets me every time. It hits on all cylinders more than any of her other songs.
