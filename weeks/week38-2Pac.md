I know this is what everyone has been waiting for - for your very white, midwestern, middle-aged man to talk about western hip-hop and gangsta rap...

* *2Pacalypse Now* (1991)
* *Strictly 4 My N.I.G.G.A.Z.* (1993) <small>second playthrough only</small>
* *Me Against the World* (1995)
* *All Eyez on Me* (1996)
* *The Don Killuminati: The 7 Day Theory* (1996)

There are many lives I have not lived in this life, and the one I have lived has an infinitesimally small crossover to the life displayed in 2Pac's songs - a fact that made it nearly impossible to relate to any single song this week. This isn't the first time that has happened, but it is the first week where the content of the songs often made it difficult to enjoy things that I do normally like. Every album was full of songs with some great beats, some grooves that I found myself digging - they energized my body, had me bobbing my head along, and I started to feel the song... and then I would notice the lyrics. I don't know where my line is, but this week taught me that there are a few things you can sing about that will put your song somewhere in the range of "I just don't care" to "I hate this" for me. It turns out I don't care to hear about:

* Any gun you own, and how you have shot someone or would shoot
* Misogynistic explanations of how many women you have slept with, while also being hypocritical and referring negatively to women who sleep with lots of men
* Your "beef" with someone else, and how much better you are than them, and what you will do to them if they try to come at you

Right there, I just outlined 75% of 2Pac's songs that I listened to this week. And I can imagine a world where I grew up in the same environment as 2Pac, and I would 1) feel a kinship, or feel seen by these songs, and/or 2) would have a completely different context and understanding of them. So I'm not really here to throw down an objective statement about any of this music. But subjectively, most of it is just not something I want to listen to; and that's ok, not all music needs to be (or should be) for me.

With all that said, there were some songs that I liked, and they fell into a few categories...

### Songs that had such a great beat that I either didn't care about the song at all, or that had lyrics that didn't shove me away

This is early songs like "If My Homie Calls", where the programming sounded great - especially the keyboards, and the different aspects to the music dropped in and out wonderfully. Or the ridiculously catchy beat in "It Ain't Easy", with the bass/drums/synth/guitar sampling playing sounding great together. And *All Eyez on Me* is swimming in these songs, with some serious earworms in "How Do U Want It", "California Love", and "I Ain't Mad At Cha". "How Do U Want It" was stuck in my head for days, K-Ci & JoJo sound great in the chorus, the guitar sampling sounds great, and the music as a whole is so catchy. "I Ain't Mad At Cha" sounds so good with the sampled DeBarge song (that I also recognize from Blackstreet's "Don't Leave Me"). And "California Love"... I'll have more to say about this later, but it goes beyond me singing "Jeremiyah Love" in my head during every Notre Dame football game the past few seasons.

### Songs that talked about social issues

This somewhat bleeds into the next category of songs, but there is a specific piece to these songs that I appreciated in multiple ways. "Brenda's Got a Baby" speaks to the life circumstances of a young woman who is pregnant in a world that didn't and doesn't support her, and has some real emotion to it - and how it affects everyone. And it does it with a really great beat. "Keep Ya Head Up" reuses the super-catchy chorus from ("O-o-h Child" by The Five Stairsteps) to great effect, and covers a wide range of real-life circumstances ranging from being black, feminism, rape culture, and more. These songs, and others like them, *felt* important when listening to them, and the music was in step with the message throughout.

### Songs that felt "more real" to me

I want to be really careful with how I talk about this last category, because - as I've stated - these songs are all written about lives that I have not lived, with context that I don't understand or know about, and life circumstances that I do not and cannot know. What I am referring to, though, are songs that felt more vulnerable, that allowed a deeper look into his experience. These songs managed to create a bridge between his life experience and mine that I was able to feel, despite the litany of differences between us, and that felt impressive to me.

"Dear Mama" really made me feel the complicated relationship and history between 2Pac and his mama, and did it with a combination of a simple guitar/bass/percussion arrangement that complemented some strong vocals with great harmonies. "Life Goes On" is a peek into the emotional/vulnerable side that goes along with his life that he sings about in other songs (the ones I didn't particularly like). Life goes on while people around him are dying, and his own contemplations of life and death - all of this with some beautiful background vocals and a beat that complements the vocals well. The piano and percussion combo drives "Krazy", an introspective song about his life, the life around him, and how he deals with all of it. All three of these songs, and others like them, got me to stop and really pay attention - during a week when I was struggling to stay engaged with many of the songs.

### So where does this leave me?

This was a complicated week. I can't deny the talent - he often layered his own vocals on top of each other to create harmonies or background vocals, and whether he was singing or rapping, he sounded good. He had a solid group of contemporaries contributing to his songs, and they worked together well. He produced some great beats that were catchy and easy to lose myself in. He has a handful of songs that grabbed me and kept me engaged. But, he had a lot of songs that I struggled to get through for reasons I've already gone through. I do not see myself purposefully revisiting any of his music, but there are a few songs I would enjoy hearing if I came across them randomly. And, if I try to step into a world where my life is different, I can see his impact as he brought emotional vulnerability into rap in a way that others had not yet, and his political commentary felt impactful and meaningful.

* Favorite Album: Easily *All Eyez on Me*, and specifically Disc 1. I actually didn't love much on Disc 2, but song after song either had a great beat I loved, a message that felt worth hearing, an emotional window into his person, or a combination of the three. This album felt like it lived up to its hype.
* Favorite Song: It probably should be one of the deeper songs, but dammit I just love "California Love". It's the one example from the week where the vocal processing works for me. The bass sounds great. The keyboards are so good. The synth strings are perfect. The music is killer, and I really get into it. I don't even know half the lyrics - and if they are bad, I just don't want to know.

Next week, my journey takes me to the Talking Heads.
