I guess I was due for an "I don’t get it" week…

* *The Velvet Underground & Nico* (1967)
* *White Light/White Heat* (1968)
* *The Velvet Underground* (1969)
* *1969: The Velvet Underground Live* (1974)
* *Loaded* (1970)

It’s not even that I hated listening to The Velvet Underground this week, it’s that I listened to four albums from Rolling Stone’s top 500 albums, and there was only one that I really liked - and two that even felt like good albums to me. While I’m aware of this Brian Eno quote, but I still don’t get it:

> The first Velvet Underground album only sold 10,000 copies, but everyone who bought it formed a band.

Let’s look first at *The Velvet Underground & Nico*. "The Velvet Underground" portion of this album is *fine* - good rhythms, strong instrumentation, and "Heroin" somehow made me feel the physical and emotional journey of being addicted to drugs despite never having lived that. And yet, Nico is not a good singer. And Lou Reed isn’t the greatest singer, but he at least worked for what the band was doing. Nico, though, is off key, pitchy, and genuinely unpleasant to listen to. She takes a few songs in "Femme Fatale" and "I’ll Be Your Mirror" and made me dislike them entirely… and yet, when I heard those songs on *1969: The Velvet Underground Live* with Lou Reed singing, I was drawn to both of them. I felt more of the potential of the album than I felt actual greatness.

Next, we have *White Light/White Heat*. It features an over 8-minute-long "song" that technically has music (bass and drums are solid), but it’s all talking. I still have no idea what’s going on in that song. And while there are a few bright spots - like the piano in "White Light/White Heat", and "Here She Comes Now" is really catchy (and the one song I enjoyed) - the album ends with "Sister Ray", a 17-minute-long song that I hated more and more as it went on. At the least, that’s two songs that take up half the runtime of the album, and *at best* I strongly disliked them. For the first time in 42 weeks, I actually considered "nope-ing" out at this point.

And then, thankfully, along came *The Velvet Underground*, which upon finishing I wrote "THIS album, I get". The entire vibe of the album felt coherent in a way that their other albums didn't. Despite feeling the shared DNA between this album and the others, there was a part of me that was thinking "was that the same band?". Three of the first four songs were some of my favorites of the week, and it came right after hating "Sister Ray" for over 17 minutes. "Candy Says" has a calming mood set by a really nice guitar arrangement and introspective lyrics. I loved the organ in "What Goes On", despite the song hanging around for a tad too long. And "Pale Blue Eyes" had some real emotion to it (something I struggled to find in Lou Reed's vocal performances at times). The music and vocals complemented each other well, and the song felt interesting to me in a way that previous albums failed to pull me in. "Jesus" has really good musical transitions, slowing down and building back up - and harmonies that worked well with all of it. "I'm Set Free" had a laid back feel that builds up well in the middle. All of these songs shared a common feel to them, and the album offered a different side to the band that was hinted at in previous albums - but they lived in it here. Give me more albums like this, and I'd feel a lot better about the band as a whole.

After that, I was in a better place to experience *1969: The Velvet Underground Live* and *Loaded*. The live album gave me a chance to hear a few songs that I liked more live (and without Nico), and what felt like an even stronger version of "Heroin". Having this live perspective made me appreciate the band more, but unfortunately didn’t do a ton for my appreciation of their first two albums - at best it made me want studio versions of "Femme Fatale" and "I’ll Be Your Mirror", which I genuinely liked with Lou Reed at the helm.

*Loaded* was a solid album, but again didn’t feel groundbreaking. I enjoyed "Rock & Roll", "New Age", and "Oh! Sweet Nuthin’" - some of which I liked live, but liked the studio album more. And I did not realize that "I’m Sticking With You" was a Velvet Underground song. It’s an adorable song that I like a lot for what it is. And the album finishes with "Ride Into the Sun", an interesting and almost vulnerable song with some great organ music that drew me in. And yet, there were also moments of discordant music and vocal performances that really didn’t work for me. *Loaded* was easily my second favorite studio album of the week, and I still felt mixed on it.

I’m open to the idea that part of my "I don't get it" feeling this week is because I'm listening to these albums 40-50 years after they were released. Maybe I've just heard the special things that The Velvet Underground did countless times already, but at the time they were groundbreaking. But I don't find myself in a place where I have enjoyed hearing the majority of their music.

* Favorite Album: No shocker here, it's *The Velvet Underground*. I enjoyed it far, far more than any of the other albums, and it's their one album that really, truly made sense to me.
* Favorite Song: There were a few candidates on the live album here, and I think if I had a studio version of them, they might stand more of a chance, but this really comes down to the studio version of "Pale Blue Eyes". More than any other song, I felt some real emotion from Lou Reed's vocal performance, and the bass and guitar really do sound great together in it. The song has a depth to it that just wasn't fully replicated in any of their other work.

Very curious as to how next week will go, with a band that - if my understanding is correct - is in a direct lineage to The Velvet Underground: Sonic Youth.
