FINALLY. After a few straight weeks of feeling more than mixed, I finally found another artist that I loved. Turns out, the cure to the hangover from a week where I adored *Rolling Stone's* #8 album of all time is to have a week with its #3 album.

* *Clouds* (1969)
* *Ladies of the Canyon* (1970)
* *Blue* (1971)
* *For the Roses* (1972) <small>second playthrough only</small>
* *Court and Spark* (1974)
* *The Hissing of Summer Lawns* (1975)
* *Hejira* (1976)
* "Both Sides Now" (2000)

While I didn't *absolutely* love everything this week, I also didn't hate anything. Some albums I really loved - I found myself writing "I could listen to this all day" with these - and the rest (even my "least favorite") I still liked. I can give an unqualified recommendation of Joni Mitchell.

With that all said, now for the qualification. *For the Roses* represented a pivot in Joni's career. Up until that point, she was distinctly folk, and *For the Roses* starts a pivot more into jazz. The folk aspect is still there and she's still a story-teller, but this album marks a shift for her. Her songs go from mostly being just her with a piano or guitar (and sometimes with one other artist playing guitar with her), to songs with multiple accompanying instruments, more "jazzy" phrasing, and other stylistic changes. And while I still like this version of Joni (or DJ Jazzy Joni, as I like to call her), it just wasn't the same. The highs were just not as high for me as they were in her earlier albums, the "wow" moments just didn't come as often. In those last three albums, I found myself pulled into songs less, missing her voice being the obvious focal point of the music. So while I enjoyed *Court and Spark*, *The Hissing of Summer Lawns*, and *Hejira*, I'm not going to talk much about them, because they weren't what made me love her music. Not what I expected coming into this week, and not what I expected after finishing *Blue* - to say "the other three albums that are considered to be in the 500 greatest albums ever were the ones that I liked *less*" - but here we are.

It's always fun when I hear previous artists I've listened to in a later artist. Things I loved about Aimee Mann? I hear Joni Mitchell and see where it came from. Same with Stevie Nicks. Joni tells a story as well or better than any artist I've ever heard - her emotional delivery, her vocal control, and the way her piano/guitar playing would swell and fade with her vocals continually drew me in to song after song. Songs like "Morning in Morgantown" is a great example - I don't care about Morgantown at all, but I wanted to listen to her story about it. In "I Don’t Know Where I Stand", she somehow managed to make not-real lyrics like "do do do do do" sound good and engaging.

Something that really impressed me was her ability to convey complicated emotions. It wasn't just the sadness or joy that came across, but also things like longing and bittersweetness. Her lyrics and vocal complexity often portrayed complicated feelings in a way that I could hear the internal struggle between love and hate in "All I Want", the journey from depression to escape back to depression in "Blue", and a masterful delivery of both sadness and hope in "Little Green". And, of course, perhaps her most famous song "Both Sides, Now" (1969) makes these feelings explicit.

Part of how she conveys these emotions is with her amazing control over her range. Take a song like "I Think I Understand", where the chorus comes in soft, gets strong in the middle, and closes softly again - drawing attention to her embracing her understanding of uncertainty and fear. Then there's "Blue", where I can almost feel the energy drain out of her as she finds herself being drawn back into depression, her escapes failing her. And "River", her vocals are a true journey in a way I can't even explain, so just go listen to it... I'll wait.

What makes these vocals so striking is that there is often just a sparse musical arrangement behind her. Sometimes it's just her with a piano or a guitar. Other times James Taylor or [Stephen Stills](https://scottpilgrim.fandom.com/wiki/Stephen_Stills) (oh, probably the other [Stephen Stills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stephen_Stills)...) is there with another guitar. These arrangements let her drive the song, to be the focal point. And more often than not, the music would meld with her vocals - a tribute to both her playing<sup>1</sup> and the artists she surrounded herself with. "Rainy Night House" is a great example of this, the piano and strings at times felt inseparable from her voice.

And while I often wanted every song to just be Joni and her piano/guitar, that's not the whole story. With about 1.5 minutes left in "For Free", a cello comes in and adds a fantastic layer, melding perfectly with her singing and piano playing. The percussion and choir in "Big Yellow Taxi" combine with her guitar playing to give a great energy to the song. And those subtle strings in "Rainy Night House" add such depth to the song, making the song feel both beautiful and a bit haunting.

I could go on and on here - the way she goes from the top of her range to the bottom with no gaps and such control, the multiple other examples of emotion coming through in her songs, her beautiful piano playing, her original guitar styles, etc. But I'll sum it up in this way - just go listen to *Clouds*, *Ladies of the Canyon*, and *Blue* (and to a *slightly* lesser extent *For the Roses* - though I will say that album closes with a pretty good 4-5 song stretch). They are great albums, you can't go wrong with any of them.

* Favorite song: "Blue"? "Little Green"? "Both Sides, Now" (1969)<sup>2</sup>? It's really almost any of those, and possibly several others depending on what song I listened to last. But the emotion of "River" combined with some great piano playing really grabbed me, and the journey she takes you on vocally is wonderful.
* Favorite album: It's *Blue*, right? But, somehow, it's also *Clouds*. The first time listening through, I really felt like I enjoyed *Clouds* more than *Blue*, and part of that was because I was putting insane pressure on *Blue* to be perfect because of how highly the album is regarded. Then, on my second listen, I decided to just sit back and "let *Blue* happen", and it really came alive for me. The dark journey of "Blue". The mixture of sadness and hope in "Little Green" (a song about her complicated feelings about giving her daughter up for adoption), and the vocal mastery of "River" really worked for me, and put it just over the top. But I do really love *Clouds*.

Thank goodness this week came along. I was really starting to feel frustrated and hopeless, that maybe I had just been listening to too much music to really enjoy music right now. The first track on *Clouds* immediately made this fear go away, and I'm so happy about it. And I'm excited to see what The Clash has to bring next week!
