A Deadhead I am not.

* *Live Dead* (1969)
* *Workingman's Dead* (1970)
* *American Beauty* (1970)
* *Europe '72* (1972)
* *Blues for Allah* (1975) <small>second playthrough only</small>
* *Terrapin Station* (1977)

I'm not going to talk about a lot of specifics this week. I'm not even going to go album by album. The biggest reason for all of this is that I would be constantly stating two facts:

* I don't like jam bands
* I don't like Country/Americana

And it's not like the Grateful Dead sit smack dab in the middle of these categories - they aren't Phish, they aren't Alan Jackson. But most of their songs sit enough in either of these categories that they bounced right off of me.

On top of that (and I feel like I'm treading into dangerous territory here), I rarely liked the vocal performances. I especially didn't like Jerry Garcia's vocal performances - and while I generally preferred Bob Weir's vocals more, even that was hit or miss. I will give them credit for their harmonies, though - they do a pretty good job of working together vocally in almost every song. And hey, if Jerry Garcia can lead this group, that should give everybody who is just an "ok singer" a ray of hope that they have a shot in the music industry too (as long as they are a very good guitar player too, I guess).

That last point brings up the saving grace of the Grateful Dead: they are all really good musicians. The guitar playing was always good - even in songs where there was so much bend that I was pushed away from the song, I could still recognize the talent. And most songs had a moment where I was noticing the guitars, bass, drums, or keyboards to enjoy some of the song, regardless of how I felt about the song as a whole.

Live albums worked *better* than the studio albums, but that still wasn't always a sure thing. Whether it was the 23-minute tracks, the country tendencies, or songs like "Feedback" (don't record your sound checks, people), most songs either droned on for me or just had me waiting for the next track.

As far as the studio albums go, there were a small number of songs that even piqued my interest, with the best of those being "Till the Morning Comes". All the vocals are harmonies and work really well. The acoustic and electric guitars work really well together, and the bass complements them well. The drums shine through in a few moments as well. For as much as the rest of the songs in the studio albums bounced off of me, this song was a winner.

If there was anything close to a highlight for me, it was a handful of songs in *Europe '72*. In general, the addition of keyboards helped almost every track on the album, taking some songs (like "Cumberland Blues") from songs I didn't like at all to "oh, hey, this isn't too bad!". The highlights, though, were "One More Saturday Night" and "Hurts Me Too", with "Brown-Eyed Woman" almost making the cut. "One More Saturday Night" had some real Chuck Berry and Jerry Lee Lewis vibes, with some great piano and guitar throughout. Even though the vocals don't rise to the level of the music, they are good enough (and the music is great enough) to make this a song worth listening to for me. "Hurts Me Too" is great right from the guitar/piano opening - and the harmonica is fantastic. The song has a few slow points, and I wish the song was a bit shorter - but I *really* like the parts of the song that I like.

I can't say I'm leaving this week with an understanding of how the Grateful Dead have become such a phenomenon. I used to work with a guy who had been to *dozens* of Grateful Dead concerts, and I'm under the impression that this is not an exception. What I find most interesting is that even though I had some moments of understanding why people would love hearing them live, I barely heard anything in the studio albums that made me feel like I would be interested in seeking them out to ever hear them live. Maybe it's because their music isn't for me, I can understand that idea - but I'm a little bummed that I am not finishing up this week with more of an "I get it" feeling.

* Favorite Album: *Europe '72* is the easy win here, because I didn't enjoy any of the studio albums, and this was the live album that had more rock-style songs that I enjoyed.
* Favorite Song: Not a lot to pull from here, but I'm going to give it to "Till the Morning Comes". I felt like it woke me from my country stupor that every previous album had put me in, and remains the lone album song that I enjoyed start to finish this week.
