Here is a list of things that I *knew* about Fleetwood Mac before this week:

* Stevie Nicks was **the** lead singer of the band
* "Landslide" is a great song
* *Rumours* is a well-regarded album, but I don't think that I've ever heard a song from it
* Mick Fleetwood is the man to go to if you ever [need a collar removed](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uBKgwApklm0&t=15s)

A lot can change in a week. Here's this week's playlist, and stay around until the end for an exclusive interview with Lindsey Buckingham!

* *Peter Green's Fleetwood Mac* (1968)
* "Albatross" (1968) <small>second playthrough only</small>

My first "bit of knowledge" went out the window immediately when I found myself in two albums (of several) that existed before Stevie Nicks was even part of the band. I was this-week-years-old when I learned that Fleetwood Mac went through band members like water before adding Stevie Nicks and Lindsey Buckingham. And those albums are... not as good.

That's not to say there weren't some things to like on *Peter Green's Fleetwood Mac* (originally just named *Fleetwood Mac* - but then they used that album title again 7 years later) and *Then Play On*. Early Fleetwood Mac was a pretty good blues band, with some nice grooves and overall solid music. But where they fell short for me, over and over again, was vocally. Whether it was Peter Green or Danny Kirwan, there just wasn't enough "there there" for me. Peter Green had some moments - I really liked "I Loved Another Woman", a song with some great guitar and bass work and engaging vocals throughout the song. But his strength and weakness for me was that his performances evoked Ray Charles for me. The positive: evoking Ray Charles means there is something good there, because I *really* loved Ray's ability to convey so much emotion with his vocal performances. The negative: by just evoking a hint of what Ray Charles brings, it showed me just how much was missing. More often than not, it sounded like he was trying to sound both emotional and cool at the same time - where Ray's natural emotional presence brought an "air of coolness" along with it. To me, what Peter ended up doing felt like he was teasing me with what could be an emotional performance, but it kept getting overridden by "listen to the grit in my voice, and how cool I sound".

Danny Kirwan mostly just fell flat for me overall - I don't have a "this is what was wrong" rundown on him, other than his vocal performances were not engaging for me. And beyond that, the harmonies often sounded bad to me ("Like Crying" being a great example where the harmonies hurt a song that was good musically). So the vocals were a huge issue for me, but the bones were there - the music was more often than not good.

Enter Stevie Nicks and Lindsey Buckingham, and 1975's *Fleetwood Mac*. Now we are going to get Stevie Nicks leading every song, right? NO! The album starts off with "Monday Morning", a song led by Lindsey Buckingham... and everything already felt better vocally. Buckingham sounded good in the lead. The harmonies were great. And the music was even better - in fact, above all, the song felt coherent; the vocals and music complemented each other the entire way, as if they belonged together.

The album continued to break my "knowledge" - as I eventually figured out that from song-to-song, the lead vocalist would be Stevie Nicks, Lindsey Buckingham, or Christine McVie. Each has its own feel, but it still feels like the same band regardless of who is singing. With that said, my favorite songs on this album were Stevie Nicks-led songs. "Rhiannon" has an amazing guitar/bass intro, and the song is ridiculously catchy. After my first playthrough, I had the chorus of this song running through my head repeatedly (despite not really knowing the lyrics, still). The music is great, and the harmonies are fantastic. And then, of course, is "Landslide". I already loved this song, and that did not change. We can add it to the "what songs make Brett cry" category, as the "children get older" line got me this week. But what a great emotional performance, with a wonderful intimate guitar arrangement.

And now we come to *Rumours*... which is an album I'll talk more about later :)

*Tusk*, "Gypsy", and *Tango in the Night* closed out the week, and whether I wanted them to be or not, they were colored by *Rumours*. These are good albums, with good songs. But they are not *Rumours*.

*Tusk* felt like a mix of experiments and a continuation of what the band had done well. The amazing harmonies were there. The music was good. And then there were songs like "The Ledge" and its weird guitar effects, or "That's All for Everyone" where the music/lead vocals/background vocals all felt at odds with each other. And yet, we still get songs like "Sara", with some great piano playing, wonderful vocals, and a moment about 40 seconds in where the music kicks in differently in a great way. Then we have "Storms", a song that builds up over the course of the entire song to complement a wonderfully emotional vocal performance. "Sisters of the Moon" was a nice surprise for me. The music and vocals complement each other so well, both doing a great job of building the song up, then settling back down, then building up again, back and forth. My favorite song of the album, though, was "Beautiful Child". The piano intro was striking. The arrangement is beautiful, the vocals are so strong and emotional, the harmonies are beyond effective, and the piano and guitar sound great together.

"Gypsy" felt like a classic Stevie Nicks led song, strong vocals that build well over the course of the song, with music to complement the performance well. The beat and music of the song was great, and it was one of the better post-Rumours songs for me.

*Tango in the Night* felt more in line with *Tusk* - a few experimental things that didn't work for me, and yet a few songs that really did work. "Little Lies" is up there with the "oh, I didn't know this is Fleetwood Mac" for me, partially because it's a Christine McVie led song. The chorus is great, the music and the vocals really kick in masterfully, with the drums, bass, guitar, and keyboard all sounding great. "Seven Wonders" was my favorite song on the album, with a great opening of synth and guitar leading into the vocals coming in. Again, the chorus sounds awesome, with the background vocals/harmonies and the music sounding so good, complementing each other well. And the keyboards/synth sound really good to me the entire song, making a great rhythm that pulled me in and kept me there.

Ok, ok, ok, so let's get back to *Rumours*. I've heard this was a great album, and I came into it worried that I would put too much pressure on it to be good. Turns out, I didn't need to worry. This is one of the best albums I've ever heard in my life. The album starts off with "Second Hand News", a song that I'd have nothing but good things to say about it if it wasn't for the "bow da bow bow doo doodily doo" portion of the lyrics. But then we get to "Dreams", and the album gets into a six-song stretch of hit after hit. And you know what? I had heard several of these before - again, I was this-week-years-old when I realized that "Dreams", "Don't Stop", "Go Your Own Way", and "The Chain" are Fleetwood Mac songs. Even the songs that didn't feel like downright hits were really good - "Gold Dust Woman" ends the album with a final minute that struck me as a killer way to end an amazing album. But let's get back to that stretch in the middle... I'll now gush over those songs:

* "Dreams" has a great beat that sets a fantastic mood. The chorus just *feels* good. Music and vocals complement each other so well through the entire song. The harmonies are perfect. The keyboards (piano, Rhodes piano, and organ) all sound really good with Nicks's vocal performance.
* Buckingham's layered vocals in "Never Going Back Again" are so effective, the star of the song, as he's singing lead and backing vocals for himself. The guitar is right there with the vocals, and the simple arrangement is striking. And not to be left out, the moments seem chosen perfectly for Nicks and McVie to come in with backing vocals.
* The piano is really good in "Don't Stop", and the rest of the music works very well with it. Buckingham and McVie both sound great vocally. And the guitar in the middle is excellent.
* "Go Your Own Way" is not, as my brain thought, a Kansas song. I think I mentally had it mixed up with "Carry On Wayward Son". Anyway, this song has such great energy, and the drums come in great early. The bass drives the song, kind of a hidden hero behind all the rest of the wonderful instrumentation. The drums and guitar crush it at the end. And the vocals, oh the vocals. The harmonies are perfect. The vocalists push when they need to push, meld when they need to meld. Top-notch vocal performances across the board.
* *Songbird* is beautiful. The piano is an amazing complement to a spectacularly emotional vocal performance. And, of course, I love love. There is just enough guitar - not too little, not too much - to add a little something to the song. And I'll say it again, the piano sounds beautiful.
* And to end the run, we have "The Chain". Don't worry, song - I love you now, so I will love you again. The drum/guitar intro is so, so good. It grabs me immediately and doesn't let me go. The harmonies are immediately effective. And then we hit the chorus. **Every time** the chorus starts and the drums kick in more, I am completely sucked into the song. This might be my single favorite Stevie Nicks background vocal performance of the week - her performance complements Buckingham and the music so well (in a way that I can't fully describe). And again, the harmonies during the chorus, with McVie providing backing vocals - shit, it's damn near haunting. Then the musical build-up after the three-minute mark happens, and the song goes on to kill it musically - every instrument gets a chance to shine and it sounds so good.

What else can I say? This album brings it from start to end, and that middle is almost unparalleled. And with that, here's what I know actually about Fleetwood Mac:

* Stevie Nicks was *a* lead singer of the band, but probably my favorite (Lindsey Buckingham is close, though)
* "Landslide" is **still** a great song
* *Rumours* earns its status as a well-regarded album. Turns out I knew several songs from it, and it's one of the best albums I've ever heard in my life. I can definitely say that I like Fleetwood Mac, but I **love** *Rumours*.
* Once we get to the Buckingham/Nicks years, they have some of the best harmonies I've heard from any band, ever
* Mick Fleetwood also plays the drums really well
* Favorite Album: No shocker here, it's *Rumours*. I think I've spelled that out enough.
* Favorite Song: Is it "Landslide"? It might be. But it also might be "The Chain". These are completely different styles of songs, and I love them both, and you can't make me choose.
