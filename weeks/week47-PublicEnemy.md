Almost. In so many ways, almost.

* *Yo! Bum Rush the Show* (1987)
* *It Takes a Nation of Millions to Hold Us Back* (1988)
* *Fear of a Black Planet* (1990)
* *Apocalypse 91... The Enemy Strikes Black* (1991)

I got really excited when I started up *Yo! Bum Rush the Show*. Both "You're Gonna Get Yours" and "Sophisticated Bitch" had some great guitar samples, some engaging turntable work, and a really catchy beat. I was immediately reminded of Run-DMC, and I really liked them when I listened to them many weeks back. However, as I continued to listen to the album, I quickly started noting where the two groups differed. Where Run-DMC would often use live instruments and mix them with programming, loops, samples, etc., Public Enemy - from what I could tell - was always using samples and programming, with little-to-no live instrumentation. And while this seems like a small difference, it trickled down into many small things that left me *almost* liking Public Enemy, but continually comparing them to Run-DMC... and Run-DMC would always win that comparison.

I'll start with the things I didn't like. By the time I got to "By the Time I Get to Arizona" on *Apocalypse 91... The Enemy Strikes Black*, I had hit a run of songs that started and/or ended with some sort of spoken word. Sometimes it sounded like clips from a newscast. Other times it was an intro presenting the song. Regardless of what it was, though, it's just never something I like or want. I have some forgiveness for it starting or ending a song - there are a few songs where I noted that "once the song gets going, I liked it" ("Can't Truss It" and "Fear of a Black Planet" are two good examples of this). When the spoken word is repeatedly worked into the song, though, it ends up breaking the flow for me over and over. This downright kills songs like "Terminator X to the Edge of Panic", "Revolutionary Generation", and "More News at 11" for me.

And then there are the songs with single, persistent, and repetitive sounds in songs. This was the biggest *almost* for me all week, and repeatedly so. There are a handful of songs that I am certain I would really like if I could just remove one sound from them. "Terminator X to the Edge of Panic" comes up again here, where a high-pitched squeal sound keeps repeating, that kills a great beat for me. There's a high-pitched "tea kettle" sound in "Rebel Without a Pause" that made me angry, because everything else about the song - especially the turntables - sounds great. "Night of the Living Baseheads", "Cold Lampin’ with Flavor", and "War at 33 1/3" are more examples of songs where I felt robbed - wondering how much I would like these songs if I could just remove one specific thing from each.

A side effect of almost always using samples was that there was a tendency for things to get repetitive. Sometimes it was the same 3-second guitar sample looping repeatedly over a 4-minute song, sometimes it was a vocal sample that would be repeated 10 straight times. It wasn't always bad (and, sometimes, actually worked for me), but it did make a few songs drag for small periods of time. The key would be how much of the rest of the song was dynamic - and for me, it was fairly mixed as to when they succeeded on that front.

And finally, we need to talk about Flavor Flav. He's fun, he's a decent rapper, and a good hype man. But outside of a few songs where I liked what he was doing, the rest of the way I just wanted him to get out of Chuck D's way. To some degree, I get his role in the group, and he does those things well - mostly being the hype man. But his energy often felt out of place in (or at odds with the) song. And don't even get me started on his laugh... I really don't like that.

But it's not all gloom here - there was still a lot to like. I'll start with Chuck D - who I think is great. His authoritative presence sets the tone for so many songs, and he's a legitimately great rapper. "Bring the Noise", "She Watch Channel Zero?!", "Welcome to the Terrordome", "Fight the Power", and "Rebirth" are all songs with good beats that are brought up a level simply by his presence in the song.

Overall, most songs have a great beat as well. Yes, I have some issues with over-sampling (repetitive, overpowering other parts of the song, etc.), but mostly the drum programming, guitar/bass/horn sampling, and turntable work were great - and at times exceptional. In fact, even with all of the things that I didn't like, I genuinely enjoyed listening to all 4 albums - at no point was I thinking "when will this album be over" like I have with some other artists. Even when I got into songs where things were repetitive, it didn't make me dislike an entire song as much as make me wish a song was a minute shorter, or introduce another more dynamic layer. All of this coalesces in me being able to say that I appreciate and mostly liked Public Enemy, but they also were only *almost* Run-DMC, so I didn't end up loving them.

* Favorite album: This was easily *It Takes a Nation of Millions to Hold Us Back*. In addition to having almost all of my favorite songs this week, it also has multiple songs that I almost really liked. Yes, it's a knock against it that there are 3-4 songs that made me frustrated that one repeated sound ruined each song. But also, if it weren't for those sounds, that would be 3-4 more songs I might be raving about.
* Favorite song: This one almost goes to a song on that very album, but instead it goes to what I see as the superior version. "Bring the Noise" on *Apocalypse 91... The Enemy Strikes Black* (featuring Anthrax) won by a clear margin. I have heard the first minute of this song countless times, as it took me back to the many, many, many hours I spent playing *Tony Hawk's Pro Skater 2* instead of studying in college. Chuck D is so strong, the mix of R&B/rock is fantastic, and the turntables are excellent. This song flat out rocks.
