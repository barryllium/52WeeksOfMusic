Sometimes you're having a rough week. You're tired, stressed, and the universe has decided that you should have too much going on. And sometimes - while in the midst of those weeks - the universe gives you Otis Redding and [totally redeems itself](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GSr-VWc_7WQ).

* "These Arms of Mine" (1962)
* "Pain In My Heart" (1964)
* "Security" (1964)
* *Otis Blue/Otis Redding Sings Soul* (1965)
* *Complete & Unbelievable: The Otis Redding Dictionary of Soul* (1966)
* *The Dock of the Bay* (1968)
* "I've Got Dreams To Remember" (1968)

It took me about two seconds to hear Ray Charles' influence on Otis Redding - the phrasing, the emotional directness, the vocal delivery. But Otis was never just a Ray Charles imitator. He took that foundation and turned it into something uniquely his own. Yes, Otis didn't have the range of talent that Ray did (piano playing and style range) - but his vocal delivery did all the things that I loved about Ray, and did them better. He was somehow more raw, more expressive, more emotional for me - and those are things that Ray was *great* at. And - as I learned this week - Otis was a spectacular songwriter. Did you know that he wrote "Respect"? I didn't before this week - I thought it was an Aretha Franklin song. But he wrote it, along with other great songs like "(Sittin' On) The Dock of the Bay", "I've Been Loving You Too Long (To Stop Now)", and "These Arms of Mine" - all classic songs that I had some basic familiarity with (despite the fact that, as this whole year-long experiment shows, I've apparently barely listened to any music my entire life 😃).

There really wasn't anything I didn't like this week. There were one or two songs that I didn't fully connect with, but even those still had something in them (the beat, the arrangement, something) that I still liked. Despite only listening to three full albums and a handful of singles, I'm fairly certain I tagged more songs as "a song that I love" than any other week - almost half of the songs (and that's a deflated number, because at some point I started being more critical, thinking "I can't favorite *every* song). The point is, he wasn't just great, he was *consistently* great.

What is it that I loved so much, you ask? First of all, thanks for asking - I really appreciate it. It really came down to three things - his vocal delivery, the rhythm/beat of his arrangements, and how the musical arrangement complemented and elevated his vocals. Across the board, song after song brought all of this to the table. Even in *Complete & Unbelievable: The Otis Redding Dictionary of Soul*, where the mix would sometimes mute his vocals in favor of the music, all of this was there. And it's notable that *Complete & Unbelievable: The Otis Redding Dictionary of Soul* was my least favorite album because of the mix, *and* I still loved it.

It didn't matter if he was singing his own song, or covering someone else's song. Yes, he wrote "Respect", but that's forever an Aretha song to me - and I still absolutely loved his original version. "My Girl" will always be a Four Tops song to me - and his version is different and fantastic. Sam Cooke's "A Change Is Gonna Come" sounds great in Otis's "Change Gonna Come". His version of "Wonderful World" blows the Herman's Hermits version that I knew out of the water. Not many things better than getting to love a new version of a song that I already like - and then on top of that I get to love new songs as well.

Whether it was a strong opening like in "Change Gonna Come" or "Security"; a restrained feel like in "I've Been Loving You Too Long (To Stop Now)" or "I Love You More Than Words Can Say"; or a gradual build like in "Try a Little Tenderness," the musical arrangement was almost always engaging. Some of my favorite moments came when songs gradually built in intensity, like the subtle bass, piano, and drum progression in "I've Been Loving You Too Long (To Stop Now)", the explosive second half of "Try a Little Tenderness", or the emotional swell in the final third of "The Glory of Love". He knew how to write a song, and he wasn't a one-trick pony - every song seemed to bring something new musically.

Sometimes the strength of the song came from the restraint shown with the musical arrangements. "These Arms of Mine" immediately won me over with its piano- and bass-led foundation, while the occasional guitar added just enough to keep the song dynamic. "Change Gonna Come" paired restrained piano, guitar, bass, and drums with Otis's vocals, using the horns sparingly but incredibly effectively whenever they entered. "You Don't Miss Your Water" may have been the best example of all, opening with a simple arrangement where the keyboards, bass, and drums perfectly complemented his voice before the horns came in at all the right times.

When the arrangements were fuller, they were just as engaging. "(Sittin' On) The Dock of the Bay" brought together piano, guitar, horns, bass, and a fantastic rhythm, with every piece fitting naturally into the song. "Down in the Valley" leaned into a great groove, with the bass and drums providing real funk while the rest of the arrangement added color and movement. "Let Me Come On Home" was another standout, built around excellent guitar, subtle piano, and a strong rhythmic foundation that gave the song its character.

The horn section and keyboards were something that constantly caught my attention. The horns always seemed to arrive at exactly the right moment to elevate a song, while the piano and organ (often played by the great Isaac Hayes) established the emotional foundation from the beginning. Whether it was the dramatic lift of the horns in "Change Gonna Come" and "Try a Little Tenderness", or the piano/organ-led atmosphere of songs like "These Arms of Mine", "Tennessee Waltz", and "I Love You More Than Words Can Say", those arrangements consistently won me over.

The real star for me, though, was his vocal delivery. It was real, raw, vulnerable, emotional, and somehow still refined. Sometimes it was all of these things at once, and other times he perfectly chose which aspects of these characteristics to lean into. You can feel the pain in his delivery in "Pain In My Heart", "I've Been Loving You Too Long (To Stop Now)", and "Tennessee Waltz". He shows he can switch back and forth between power and restraint in songs like "Respect", "(Sittin' On) The Dock of the Bay", and especially in "Try a Little Tenderness". And then there are the songs where his voice grabs my attention immediately by sheer force of will: "Change Gonna Come" is the perfect example of this - I stopped what I was doing and turned my head towards my phone about 30 seconds into the song because of how his vocals paired with the piano at that moment.

All of this is to say that I absolutely loved listening to Otis Redding. One of my favorite vocalists I've listened to all year, with one of my favorite albums of the year as well. More than anything, though, what impressed me was how cohesive everything felt. The arrangements weren't simply good, and the vocals weren't simply great—they constantly elevated one another. The music knew when to step forward, when to step back, and when to simply support the emotion that Otis was conveying. By the end of the week, it felt like every part of every song was working toward the same goal.

* Favorite Album: every album is great, but *Otis Blue/Otis Redding Sings Soul* is up there with *Rumours* as the best album I've heard all year. Just hearing him sing in several songs made me smile (even a sad song) because he is so consistently great - overall, but especially in this album. Cover or original, he crushes every song here.
* Favorite Song: ask me on a different day, and I'd say "Change Gonna Come" or "(Sittin' On) The Dock of the Bay". Both are amazing songs that I love. But today, it goes to "I've Been Loving You Too Long (To Stop Now)". The tenderness - and almost pain - in his voice is perfect. The restrained music works really well with his vocals. The bass/piano build-up just before the 2-minute mark is almost too subtle to notice, but it's great - and then the drums/horns build up after that, and the song gets even better. This song is pretty much the confluence of everything I love about Otis Redding - the perfect vocals, with the perfect arrangement to highlight every piece of it.
